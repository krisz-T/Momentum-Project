Software Architectures - Project Work
1. Requirements
The project work is your opportunity to demonstrate architectural thinking in practice. You will
design and build a small but complete software system, making conscious and documented
decisions about its architecture. The focus is not on the amount of features, but on the
quality of your architectural choices, how well you document them, and how effectively you
use AI-assisted development.
Each student selects one project from the list below (or proposes a custom project with
instructor approval) and works on it individually throughout the semester.
2. Minimum Scope & Complexity
Your project must meet the following minimum requirements to be considered complete.
These define the baseline – exceeding them is encouraged but not required for a passing
grade.
2.1 Functional Requirements
● At least 5 distinct features (e.g., in an expense tracker: add expense, split calculation,
settlement history, recurring expenses, export report).
● At least 2 user roles or interaction modes (e.g., organizer vs. attendee, admin vs.
regular user, quiz creator vs. quiz taker).
● Persistent data storage – the application must store and retrieve data across
sessions (database, file-based storage, or equivalent).
● A working, interactive user interface (web, mobile, or desktop – no CLI-only projects).
2.2 Architectural Requirements
● Clear layered architecture with separation of concerns (e.g., presentation, business
logic, data access).
● Feature decomposition documented in a structured way.
● State management strategy chosen and justified for the frontend.
● An OpenAPI specification for the backend API (if applicable) or equivalent interface
contract.
● Error taxonomy – categorized error types with consistent, meaningful error
responses.
● Basic security considerations: input validation, authentication for at least one
protected resource, and a simple threat model.
● At least 3 Architecture Decision Records (ADRs) documenting key decisions.
2.3 What You Do NOT Need
You do not need real deployment to a cloud provider, payment integration, real-time features
(WebSockets), complex DevOps pipelines, or a mobile-native app. A locally runnable
application with a clear README on how to start it is perfectly sufficient. Focus your energy
on architecture, not infrastructure.
3. Vibe Coding & AI-Assisted Development
This course embraces AI-assisted development as a core methodology. You are expected to
use GitHub Copilot Pro (provided to you) and/or other AI tools (e.g., ChatGPT, Claude) to
help build your project. However, using AI effectively is a skill – and that skill is being
evaluated.
What “Awareness of AI use” means (15 points):
• Maintain a prompt log: keep a record of significant prompts you used and the AI’s
responses (a simple Markdown file is fine).
• Document your evaluation process: when the AI generated code or architectural
suggestions, did you accept, modify, or reject them? Why?
• Show critical thinking: demonstrate that you understood what the AI produced, not
just copy-pasted it blindly.
• Reflect on AI limitations: note at least one case where the AI was wrong or unhelpful,
and explain how you handled it.
The goal is not to minimize AI use – it is to maximize conscious, deliberate AI use. Blind
copy-paste with no understanding will score poorly; thoughtful collaboration with AI will score
well.
4. Deliverables
Your final submission (due May 10, 2026) must include:
• Source code in the GitHub Classroom repository with meaningful commit history.
• README.md with setup instructions (how to install and run the project locally).
• Architecture documentation: at least 3 ADRs, feature decomposition, and a high-level
architecture diagram.
• OpenAPI specification (or equivalent interface contract).
• Error taxonomy document describing your error categories and handling strategy.
• Threat model (can be a simple document or diagram identifying key threats and
mitigations).
• AI prompt log with evaluation notes.
Presentation: On May 11, 2026, you will present your project in class. The presentation
should be a short live demo (5–10 minutes) focusing on your architectural decisions and how
they shaped the final product.

7. List of potential project topics
1. PantryPal – Smart Kitchen Inventory An application that helps users track what's in
their kitchen, suggests recipes based on available ingredients, and generates shopping lists
for missing items.
2. SkillSwap – Peer-to-Peer Skill Exchange A marketplace where users can offer skills
they have (e.g., guitar lessons) and request skills they want to learn, coordinating sessions
and leaving reviews.
3. SplitWise Lite – Group Expense Tracker An app for groups of friends or roommates to
log shared expenses, automatically calculate who owes whom, and track settlement history.
4. StudyBuddy – Collaborative Study Planner A tool for university students to organize
study groups, share notes, schedule study sessions, and track progress toward exam
preparation goals.
5. GreenScore – Personal Carbon Footprint Tracker Users log daily activities
(transportation, meals, energy use) and the app estimates their carbon footprint, sets
reduction goals, and visualizes trends over time.
6. BookCircle – Neighborhood Book Lending A platform for a local community to list
books they own and are willing to lend, request borrows, and manage lending periods and
returns.
7. FitQuest – Gamified Workout Tracker A fitness app where users log workouts, earn XP
and badges for consistency, compete on leaderboards, and follow structured training plans.
8. MealPrep Pro – Weekly Meal Planner An application that lets users plan their meals for
the week, automatically aggregates ingredients into a shopping list, and tracks nutritional
intake.
9. PetCare – Pet Health & Routine Manager Pet owners can track vaccinations, vet
appointments, feeding schedules, medication reminders, and share care responsibilities with
family members.
10. FreelanceFlow – Freelancer Project Dashboard A tool for freelancers to manage
clients, track project milestones, log billable hours, generate simple invoices, and monitor
income over time.
11. CampusMarket – Student Marketplace A buy-and-sell platform for university students
to list used textbooks, electronics, furniture, and other items, with in-app messaging and
meetup coordination.
12. TravelLog – Trip Planning & Journal Users can plan upcoming trips with itineraries
and budgets, then document their experiences with photos, notes, and location pins during
and after the trip.
13. VolunteerHub – Volunteer Opportunity Board Organizations can post volunteer
opportunities and volunteers can browse, sign up, track their hours, and receive certificates
for their contributions.
14. HabitForge – Habit Tracker & Accountability An app where users define habits they
want to build or break, log daily check-ins, visualize streaks, and optionally pair up with an
accountability partner.
15. QuizForge – Custom Quiz Builder A platform where users can create custom quizzes
on any topic, share them via link, and track respondents' scores and statistics. Supports
multiple question types (multiple choice, true/false, open-ended) and timed quiz modes.
+1. Custom Project – Your Own Idea Students may propose their own project idea, subject
to instructor approval. The project must be of comparable scope and complexity to the
options above.
